## Part 1: What Is PAWS and Why Does It Matter?

### The Plain Truth

You survived detox. The shaking stopped. The nausea passed. The medical crisis is over. And now — weeks or months into recovery — you feel terrible in ways nobody warned you about. Your sleep is wrecked. Your mood swings for no reason. You can't concentrate. Small things make you furious. Nothing feels good. And you're starting to wonder if recovery is even working.

**It is working. What you're feeling is your brain healing.**

Post-Acute Withdrawal Syndrome — PAWS — is what happens after the acute detox is over. Your brain spent months or years running on a substance. It rewired itself around that substance. Now that the substance is gone, your brain has to rebuild. That rebuilding takes time — anywhere from 6 months to 2 years depending on what you used and for how long.

The symptoms you're experiencing are not weakness. They are not failure. They are not a sign that you should give up. They are the sound of repair work happening inside your skull. And knowing that — knowing what's coming, why it's happening, and what to do about it — is the single most protective thing anyone can give you right now.

### The Seven Hurdles

PAWS shows up as seven clusters of symptoms. Not everyone gets all seven. Which ones hit hardest depends on what substance you used, how long you used it, and how many times you've been through this before. Your worker can help you identify which ones are most active for you.

**Hurdle 1 — Cognitive Fog**
Can't concentrate. Poor short-term memory. Slow processing. Difficulty making simple decisions. This is your prefrontal cortex rebuilding — the part of your brain responsible for thinking clearly was suppressed during active use and needs months to come back online.

*Highest risk if your primary substance was: alcohol, benzodiazepines, poly-substance.*

**Hurdle 2 — Emotional Lability**
Mood swings that come from nowhere. Crying without a clear reason. Irritability that's way out of proportion to the situation. Sudden anger or sadness. Your dopamine and serotonin systems are recovering — the brain's emotional regulation circuits were hijacked by the substance and are rebuilding baseline function.

*Affects everyone regardless of substance.*

**Hurdle 3 — Sleep Disruption**
Insomnia. Fragmented sleep. Vivid dreams. Night sweats. Early waking. Sleep that doesn't feel restful even when you get enough hours. Your GABA and glutamate systems are rebalancing — the brain's sedation and excitation systems are recalibrating. Alcohol and benzodiazepines cause particularly severe sleep disruption in PAWS.

*Highest risk if your primary substance was: alcohol, benzodiazepines, stimulants.*

**Hurdle 4 — Stress Sensitivity**
Minor inconveniences feel catastrophic. Disproportionate anger at small delays, noise, or interruption. Feeling overwhelmed by tasks that used to be manageable. Your stress response system (HPA axis) is hypersensitive — cortisol reactivity is elevated. Your nervous system overreacts to things a regulated brain would absorb without noticing.

*Highest risk if your primary substance was: opioids, stimulants.*

**Hurdle 5 — Cravings**
Sudden, intense urges to use. Triggered by places, people, smells, emotions, or seemingly nothing at all. Physical sensations: gut tightening, mouth watering, restlessness. Your brain built superhighways connecting distress to substance use. Those highways don't disappear when you stop using — they slowly become overgrown through disuse. But until they do, the pull is real and it's strong.

*Affects everyone. Conditioned pathways are universal.*

**When a craving hits, ask yourself three questions:**
1. What does the substance do that I need right now? (Calm? Numbness? Energy? Connection? Relief from pain?)
2. What would need to be true in my life for that need to be met another way?
3. What is the smallest step toward that right now?

This is not a trick. This is craving analysis — understanding what the craving is actually asking for, so you can meet the real need instead of the fake solution.

**Hurdle 6 — Anhedonia**
Inability to feel pleasure from things that used to feel good. Flatness. Emotional numbness. "Nothing feels good." Your dopamine receptors were flooded by the substance for so long that they turned down their sensitivity. Natural rewards — food, music, exercise, connection — feel insufficient by comparison. This is temporary. The receptors come back. But it takes time.

*Highest risk if your primary substance was: stimulants, opioids.*

**Hurdle 7 — Identity Confusion**
"Who am I without the substance?" Your neural pathways associated with who you are had the substance woven into them. Removing the substance doesn't automatically provide a new identity. The brain has a structural void where your self-concept used to be organised around use.

*Affects everyone. Weighted higher if you've had 2 or more prior recovery attempts.*

**For the person whose identity was also tied to a professional role (healthcare worker, first responder, caregiver):** "The person who needed help is not a contradiction of the competent professional. It is the same person, more completely seen."

### The PAWS Wave — It's Not a Straight Line

PAWS doesn't follow a straight line down. Symptoms cycle. You'll have good days followed by bad days. Good weeks followed by hard weeks. This wave pattern is normal. Understanding it matters because without knowing this, you'll interpret a bad day after a good week as evidence that recovery is failing. It's not. It's the wave.

| Period | What's Typical | Biggest Risk | What You Need |
|--------|---------------|-------------|---------------|
| **Weeks 1–2** | Acute symptoms resolving. Relief. Possible "pink cloud" optimism. | Overconfidence. Reducing structure too early. "I've got this." | Structure. Routine. External scaffolding. Not insight — scaffolding. |
| **Weeks 3–6** | PAWS emerges. Sleep disruption, irritability, fog. Pink cloud fades. | Disillusionment. "Recovery isn't working." | Psychoeducation: this is normal. It's supposed to feel like this. Basic tools. |
| **Weeks 6–12** | Symptom cycling begins. Good days then bad days. Emotional swings peak. Cravings may intensify. | Isolation. Shame about symptoms returning. "Everyone else is doing better." | Normalisation. Peer support. Somebody who says "me too." |
| **Months 3–6** | Gradual improvement with periodic setbacks. Cognition returning. Emotional range expanding. | Complacency. "I'm fine now." Reducing support prematurely. | Continued structure. Data showing real progress over time. |
| **Months 6–12** | Stabilisation. Symptoms less frequent, less intense. Identity shift beginning. | External triggers (relationships, anniversaries, environments). Overconfidence again. | Relapse reframe tools. Something to become, not just something to avoid. |
| **Months 12–24** | Integration. New neural pathways forming. Internal regulation developing. You are becoming someone different. | Life stressors (job loss, relationship breakdown) without adequate coping infrastructure. | Long-term community. Meaning. Purpose. Real sources of dopamine, oxytocin, serotonin, and endorphins. |

**The critical insight: PAWS is not a sign that recovery is failing. PAWS is the sound of your brain healing. The symptoms are the repair work.**

## Part 2: How Relapse Actually Works — The CENAPS Model

Terence Gorski's CENAPS model is the most widely used relapse prevention framework in North America. Its key insight: relapse is not a single event (the moment you use). Relapse is a process that unfolds through predictable, observable, interruptible stages.

If it's a process, it can be detected early. If it can be detected early, it can be interrupted. And the tools for interruption don't need to be clinical — they need to be accessible, portable, and in your hands during the hours when relapse actually begins.

### The Three Stages

**Stage 1 — Emotional Relapse**
You're not thinking about using. You're not planning anything. But your emotions and behaviours are setting up the conditions. Irritability, anxiety, isolation, poor self-care, disrupted sleep, skipping meetings, bottling emotions. You often don't recognise this stage because there's no conscious thought about substances. It's pre-cognitive — the process has started before you have a single thought about using.

*This is where intervention is most effective and least dramatic.*

**Stage 2 — Mental Relapse**
Now you're in internal conflict. Part of you wants to use. Part of you doesn't. Bargaining begins. "It wasn't that bad." "I could have just one." "I'll control it this time." You're thinking about people, places, and things connected to use. You're romanticising the past. You're selectively not disclosing to your worker or your support network. You're lying to yourself.

*This is where the 12 Blocks provide the most concrete interruption tools.*

**Stage 3 — Physical Relapse**
You use. Once mental relapse reaches the tipping point, physical relapse often follows quickly. The window between deciding to use and using can be minutes. Driving to the dealer. Picking up the phone. Walking into the bar.

*By this stage, the window for non-clinical interruption has closed. Everything in this book targets Stages 1 and 2.*

## Part 3: Where Current Tools Fall Short

CENAPS is sound. The science is established. The problem is delivery. Here's what's missing in the current system — and what this book was built to fill:

**1. PAWS psychoeducation is insufficient.** Most programs mention PAWS briefly. Clients are told "it gets better" without being taught what the symptoms are, why they occur, or how long they last. → This book provides a full psychoeducation module with a timeline the person can track against.

**2. Warning signs are taught but not operationalised.** Clients can list Gorski's warning signs. But there's no portable tool mapping each sign to a specific, concrete action. → The 12 Blocks provide a block-by-block interruption map: when you see this, do this.

**3. Follow-up is fragmented or absent.** Post-discharge follow-up is typically limited to outpatient appointments. Miss one and nothing happens. → The follow-up system in Section 5 provides a structured check-in timeline with a specific person who knows you.

**4. Shame accelerates relapse.** When a person experiences early relapse signals, the cultural message is failure. Shame drives concealment. Concealment accelerates the process. → The relapse reframe replaces shame with learning: "What did the setback teach you?" not "Why did you fail?"

**5. Tools require a therapist.** Most relapse prevention tools are delivered in clinical settings. They're not designed for a person alone at 2am. → Every tool in this book works without another person present. Practice cards. Breathing. A journal. Your phone.

**6. The body is ignored.** CENAPS is primarily cognitive. Warning signs are described in behavioural terms. But the body signals first — jaw tightening, gut churning, chest heaviness. These signals come hours or days before the behavioural signs. → Somatic awareness is built into every Block and every tool.

**7. No neurochemical replacement.** The person is told to stop using but not given a replacement source for what the substance was providing. → The D.O.S.E. architecture provides real replacement: Dopamine through mastery and completion. Oxytocin through connection and community. Serotonin through rhythm and recognition. Endorphins through physical exertion and genuine laughter.

## Part 4: The 12 Blocks — Anticipated Unconscious Traps

The 12 Blocks are the predictable stages of the relapse process, adapted from Gorski's CENAPS warning sign phases and mapped to G.A.P.S. interruption tools. They are sequential but not inevitable. Each one can be interrupted if you know it's coming.

The purpose is not prediction. It is preparation. When you know what's coming, you can meet it before it arrives.

### The G.A.P.S. Connection

Each Block is interrupted by one of the four G.A.P.S. capacities. These capacities map to MI's guiding spirit — the same principles your worker uses in conversation are the same principles you practise on your own:

| G.A.P.S. Capacity | What It Does | MI Spirit Equivalent |
|-------------------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Gratitude** | Interrupts cortisol. Redirects from threat-scanning to resource-noticing. | Partnership — seeing strengths together |
| **Acceptance** | Creates observer distance. See the pattern without being dragged by it. | Acceptance — without judgement |
| **Presence** | Anchors in the body, in this moment, before acting. | Compassion — creating space to serve real needs |
| **Surrender** | Releases the need to control the outcome. Trusts the process. | Empowerment — the client finds their own way |

---

### Block 1: The Drift
**CENAPS Stage:** Emotional Relapse
**What it looks like:** Subtle attitude shift. Skipping small routines. "I don't need to do that today." Internal permission to let structure slide. Missing one meeting. Then two. Then it doesn't seem to matter.
**What your body does:** Energy feels flat. You don't notice the shift because it's gradual.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Gratitude:** Name one specific thing from today that went okay. Not a big thing. A small thing. "I ate breakfast." "I answered a text." "I noticed the sky." This breaks the scarcity loop that powers the drift. Reconnect to rhythm.
**Pocket version:** *Drifting? Name one good thing. Right now. Out loud if you can.*

---

### Block 2: The Mask
**CENAPS Stage:** Emotional Relapse
**What it looks like:** Presenting as "fine" while internal distress rises. Social withdrawal begins. You stop sharing in group or with your buddy. Your answers get shorter. People ask how you are and you say "good" when you're not.
**What your body does:** Jaw tightens. Shoulders creep up. Breathing gets shallow. You're holding something and your body knows it.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Acceptance:** Fish Bowl Method. "There's something I'm not saying." Name the fish. You don't have to tell anyone else yet. Just see it swimming. Don't follow it. Don't judge it. Just notice it's there.
**Pocket version:** *Performing "fine"? There's a fish you're not naming. What is it?*

---

### Block 3: The Build
**CENAPS Stage:** Emotional Relapse
**What it looks like:** Irritability increasing. Sleep disrupting again. Small conflicts escalating. Frustration tolerance dropping. PAWS symptoms flaring. Everything feels like too much.
**What your body does:** Tension in the neck, shoulders, hands. Stomach churning. Heart rate up. The body is running hot.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Presence:** Sacred Pause at the first somatic signal. Stop. Breathe (4 in, 2 hold, 6 out, three rounds). Listen to what the body is actually saying. Align: is what I'm about to do fear-based or choice-based? Act from the answer.
**Pocket version:** *Body running hot? STOP. Breathe 4-2-6 three times. Then decide.*

---

### Block 4: The Wall
**CENAPS Stage:** Emotional Relapse
**What it looks like:** Refusing help. "I can do this alone." "I don't need the program." Rejecting structure. Pushing away the people who are trying to stay close. Cancelling appointments. Isolating by choice.
**What your body does:** Rigid posture. Arms crossed. Everything feels like an intrusion.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Surrender:** "I don't have to have the answer right now. I don't have to do this alone to prove I'm strong. Accepting help is not weakness — it's the bravest thing I've done since Day 1."
**Pocket version:** *Pushing everyone away? That's the wall. You don't have to know the answer. Just don't lock the door.*

---

### Block 5: The Fog
**CENAPS Stage:** Mental Relapse
**What it looks like:** Romanticising past use. "It wasn't that bad." "I had some good times." Memory editing — the brain highlights the relief and erases the consequences. Minimising. Glamourising.
**What your body does:** A softening. Almost nostalgic. The danger is that it feels pleasant.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Gratitude:** Reality check. Grab a pen. Write down what actually happened. Not what the fog says happened. What actually happened. The hospital visit. The lost job. The look on your kid's face. The truth is the antidote to the fog.
**Pocket version:** *Thinking "it wasn't that bad"? Write down what actually happened. All of it.*

---

### Block 6: The Bargain
**CENAPS Stage:** Mental Relapse
**What it looks like:** "I could have just one." "I'll control it this time." "Other people can use normally — why can't I?" "Just on weekends." "Just beer, not the hard stuff." The internal negotiation.
**What your body does:** Restlessness. Energy rises. The body is already preparing for the thing the mind is negotiating.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Acceptance:** Name the fish. "There's a bargain swimming in the bowl. I see it. I don't have to follow it. It's just a fish." The act of naming the bargain as a thing you're observing — rather than a reasonable idea you're considering — takes away its power.
**Pocket version:** *Bargaining? That's a fish. Name it: "There's a bargain swimming." Watch it. Don't follow it.*

---

### Block 7: The Plan
**CENAPS Stage:** Mental Relapse
**What it looks like:** Mapping access to substances. You know where to go. You know who to call. You might not have made the decision yet, but the logistics are in place. Driving past the old neighbourhood. Keeping a dealer's number in your phone. "Just in case."
**What your body does:** Hyper-alert. Focused. The survival brain has taken over and it's good at planning.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Presence:** HALT check right now. Which one is it — Hungry, Angry, Lonely, or Tired? Then call the number on your card. Not tomorrow. Now. Tell them: "I'm at Block 7. I need to talk."
**Pocket version:** *Planning access? HALT check NOW. Then call your person. Say: "I'm at Block 7."*

---

### Block 8: The Secret
**CENAPS Stage:** Mental Relapse
**What it looks like:** Lying about recovery. Hiding warning signs from your worker, your sponsor, your family. Selective disclosure — telling people enough to seem okay but holding back the parts that matter. The secret grows heavier the longer you carry it.
**What your body does:** Stomach tight. Avoiding eye contact. The weight of concealment is physical.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Surrender:** Share one true thing with one safe person. It doesn't have to be everything. It doesn't have to be perfect. One true sentence. "I'm not doing as well as I've been saying." That one sentence breaks the seal. Shame cannot survive being spoken aloud to someone who doesn't flinch.
**Pocket version:** *Hiding? Tell one person one true thing. Just one sentence. Shame can't survive daylight.*

---

### Block 9: The Tunnel
**CENAPS Stage:** Behavioural Relapse
**What it looks like:** Option reduction. "Nothing will help." Can't see alternatives. The world narrows to two choices: use or suffer. Everything else feels impossible or pointless. Learned helplessness activated.
**What your body does:** Heavy. Collapsed. Energy gone. The body has given up before the mind admits it.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Gratitude:** Micro-Win. Pick one thing. The smallest possible thing. Something that takes less than five minutes and that you can actually complete. Drink water. Step outside. Send one text. Make the bed. Complete it. Notice that you completed it. That noticing is a dopamine restart. Small is how it starts.
**Pocket version:** *Can't see a way out? Do ONE thing. The smallest thing. Finish it. Notice you finished it.*

---

### Block 10: The Permission
**CENAPS Stage:** Behavioural Relapse
**What it looks like:** "I deserve this." "Just this once." "After everything I've been through." Final rationalisation. The internal argument is over. The decision feels made.
**What your body does:** Calm — a dangerous calm. The fight is over internally. The body is ready to move.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Acceptance:** The 20-minute craving window. Research shows most cravings peak and pass within 20 minutes (Marlatt & Donovan, 2005). You do not have to fight the craving. You have to outlast it. Set a timer. Ride it. Breathe. Watch the fish. It will pass. Not because you're strong enough. Because that's what cravings do.
**Pocket version:** *"I deserve this"? Set a timer for 20 minutes. Ride it. Cravings peak and pass. You just have to outlast it.*

---

### Block 11: The Edge
**CENAPS Stage:** Physical Relapse
**What it looks like:** Procurement behaviour. You are moving toward substances. Driving. Walking. Dialling. This is no longer mental — this is physical motion toward use.
**What your body does:** Autopilot. The body is moving before the mind fully registers what's happening.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Presence:** Call the number on your card. NOW. Not in five minutes. Now. Tell them where you are. Tell them what you're doing. "I'm at Block 11. I need help right now." This is what your emergency contact is for.
**Pocket version:** *Moving toward it? CALL YOUR NUMBER. NOW. "I'm at Block 11. Help me."*

---

### Block 12: The Fall
**CENAPS Stage:** Physical Relapse
**What it looks like:** Use occurs.
**What your body does:** Relief — temporary, familiar, and followed by everything that comes after.
**G.A.P.S. Interruption — Surrender:** The Return Protocol.

This is not failure. This is data. You are not starting over. You are starting from experience.

Three questions:
1. What was the setback trying to tell me? What need was I trying to meet?
2. What did I learn about my pattern that I didn't know before?
3. What is one thing I'll do differently in the next 24 hours?

Write the answers down. Share them with your worker when you're ready. This is the Gifts From The G.A.P.S. practice — extracting the lesson from the fall so the fall becomes information, not identity.

**You are not broken. You are not back to zero. You are a person who learned something the hard way. Welcome back. We saved your seat.**

**Pocket version:** *Used? Not failure — data. What was the need? What did you learn? What's one thing different tomorrow? Welcome back.*

## Part 5: The Follow-Up System

Recovery doesn't happen in appointments. It happens between them. The follow-up system provides structured contact at predictable intervals — maintained by a specific person (the worker, a designated contact, or a peer) who knows you.

### Check-In Schedule

| Timepoint | Method | Focus |
|-----------|--------|-------|
| Day 1 (post-onboarding) | Text or call | "How was last night? Did you use the plan?" |
| Day 3 | Text or call | 72-hour plan check. "Did you do the one thing?" |
| Day 7 | Call or in-person | First HALT check review. Which hurdles are showing up? |
| Day 14 | In-person | PAWS positioning review. Adjust hurdle weights. Introduce next tools. |
| Day 30 | In-person | First arc review. What patterns are visible? Block history. |
| Day 60 | In-person | Mid-PAWS check. Cycling should be evident. Normalise the wave. |
| Day 90 | In-person | Quarter review. Cognitive function returning? Tool usage patterns? |
| Month 6 | In-person | Stabilisation check. Identity work beginning? D.O.S.E. sources in place? |
| Month 9 | In-person or call | Long-range check. External trigger review. Support network audit. |
| Month 12 | In-person | One-year review. Full arc. Where have they been? Where are they now? |
| Month 18 | Call or in-person | Integration check. New neural pathways establishing? |
| Month 24 | In-person | PAWS resolution review. The person they're becoming is visible. |

### Consent

Every check-in is governed by the Saint Christopher Handshake. The client chooses what they share. The worker sees what the client consents to. If the client goes quiet — missing two scheduled check-ins without contact — the worker reaches out once, gently. "Just checking in. No pressure. The door's open." If there's still no response, the Prodigal Son Protocol applies: the seat stays saved. They come back when they come back.

### Crisis Exception

If the client activates the crisis button (routes to 988, local crisis line, or 911), the event is logged and the worker is notified regardless of consent settings. This is the only override. It is disclosed at onboarding: "If you ever hit the crisis button, I'll know. That's the one thing I'll always see. Everything else is your choice."

## Part 6: The Five-Minute Toolkit — Pocket Reference

These tools fit in your pocket. Each takes five minutes or less. Each is grounded in neuroscience. None require anything you don't already have.

**Tool 1 — HALT Check (30 seconds)**
Rate each 1–5: Hungry/Hurt? Angry? Lonely? Tired? If any hits 4+, that's the thing to address before anything else. You can't think your way out of a problem your body is creating.

**Tool 2 — One-Breath Reset (10 seconds)**
Breathe in for 4. Hold for 2. Out for 6. Notice one thing around you. Ask: what do I actually need right now? The extended exhale activates your parasympathetic nervous system. This isn't meditation. This is physiology.

**Tool 3 — 5-4-3-2-1 Ground (2 minutes)**
5 things you can see. 4 you can hear. 3 you can feel. 2 you can smell. 1 you can taste. This moves brain activity from the emotional centre back to the thinking centre. Not distraction — neurological re-anchoring.

**Tool 4 — Name It to Tame It (30 seconds)**
Naming an emotion reduces its intensity by about half (Siegel). Instead of "I feel terrible" → "There's shame swimming in the bowl." Instead of "I want to use" → "There's a craving. It's just a fish. I'm watching it." You are not your feelings. You are the one who notices them.

**Tool 5 — Micro-Win (5 minutes)**
When everything feels impossible, pick one completable task under five minutes. Drink water. Step outside. Send one text. Complete it. Notice you completed it. That's a dopamine restart.

**Tool 6 — Connection Text (10 seconds)**
One tap. Pre-written message to your chosen person: "Having a hard moment. Don't need to talk. Just reaching out." Isolation feeds the survival brain. Connection feeds everything else.

**Tool 7 — Sacred Pause (3 minutes)**
Stop. Breathe (4-2-6, three rounds). Listen — what does the body actually need? Align — is what I'm about to do fear-based or choice-based? Act from the answer.

**Tool 8 — Snap Practice (60 seconds)**
Anticipate — "This was always going to come." See — look at the thought as an object. Shrink — make it smaller. Snap — physical reset (snap a band, clap, stamp). Throw — release it. Watch it leave.

## Part 7: For the Worker — Clinical Notes

### How This Book Integrates with MI

This book is MI made operational. The four G.A.P.S. capacities align directly to MI's guiding spirit:
- **Gratitude ↔ Partnership** — both shift from deficit to resource
- **Acceptance ↔ Acceptance** — MI says "I see you without judgement"; G.A.P.S. says "I see myself without judgement"
- **Presence ↔ Compassion** — you cannot serve someone's best interest on autopilot; presence is the mechanism
- **Surrender ↔ Empowerment** — the worker surrenders the expert role; the client surrenders the shame story; both step into trust

When using this book alongside MI practice, the worker is not prescribing — they are presenting the PAWS information and the 12 Blocks, then letting the client identify which hurdles and which Blocks feel true for them. This is the client configuring their own recovery — MI's empowerment principle in action.

### CENAPS Integration Notes

Blocks 1–4 correspond to Gorski's Emotional Relapse phase. Intervention here is most effective because the client is not yet thinking about using. The worker watches for what the client is NOT saying — withdrawal from sharing, shorter answers, skipped routines. The app's mismatch alert (worker flags a Block the client hasn't self-reported) is designed to make this invisible pattern visible.

Blocks 5–8 correspond to Mental Relapse. The internal negotiation has begun. The worker's goal is to create space for the client to name what's happening without shame. MI's "rolling with resistance" principle applies — don't push back against the bargaining. Name it. "Sounds like there might be a bargain swimming in the bowl. Want to look at it together?"

Blocks 9–12 correspond to Behavioural and Physical Relapse. Blocks 9–11 require immediate worker response. Block 12 requires the Return Protocol — no shame, no "starting over" language, extraction of learning.

### D.O.S.E. Replacement Architecture

When assigning strategies, consider which neurochemical the substance was providing and match the replacement:

| Neurochemical | What the Substance Provided | Healthy Replacement |
|---------------|----------------------------|-------------------|
| **Dopamine** | Progress, reward, "I did it" | Micro-wins, skill building, completable tasks, exercise |
| **Oxytocin** | Connection, warmth, belonging | Peer support, recovery meetings, Connection Text, buddy system |
| **Serotonin** | Status, rhythm, "I matter" | Consistent routine, recognition of progress, earned trust |
| **Endorphins** | Relief, joy, "I can breathe" | Physical exertion, genuine laughter, nature immersion, challenge survived |
