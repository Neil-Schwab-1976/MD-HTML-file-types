**This is the worker's field manual. It tells you what to do, in what order, in the 90 minutes between a person walking through the door and walking out with a plan.**

---

## The Stance

"The shore is closer than you think."

Regulate first. Document second. Act together. The person in front of you is not a case number. They are a human being who just did the bravest thing they've done in months — they reached out. Name that truth before you do anything else.

**The first words out of your mouth:** "You reached out. That's brave. I'm proud of you."

This is not a script. It is a stance. It lowers panic, opens the nervous system, and creates the two things a person in crisis needs most: safety and being seen.

### Core Principles

**Guide, don't drag.** You are a lifeguard, not a rescuer. You help them float — they choose their own shore.

**Patterns, not pathologies.** Crisis behaviours — avoidance, panic, shutting down, self-blame — are not failures. They are survival strategies shaped early in life. Name them as evidence of strength that can now be redirected.

**Be the Lighthouse, not the rescue boat.** Hold steady. Shine the light. Don't jump in and drown alongside them.

**Stabilise first, document second.** The nervous system must be regulated before the brain can process information, make decisions, or participate in planning. Every agency that puts paperwork before the person has this backwards.

**The client is the expert on themselves.** This aligns directly with MI's guiding spirit — Partnership, Acceptance, Compassion, Empowerment. You use your knowledge to guide. They use their life to choose.

**Consent first. Human-led. Always.** Nothing happens without their yes. The Prodigal Son Protocol applies at every stage: anyone may pause, leave, or return without penalty, guilt, or paperwork.

## The 90-Minute Protocol

### Minutes 0–10: Co-Regulate and Orient

Breathe with them. Not at them. With them. Match their breathing rate, then slowly extend your exhale. Their nervous system will follow yours. This is co-regulation — the most powerful tool you have, and it costs nothing.

**Sequence:**
1. Name their courage: "You reached out. That's brave."
2. Explain the 90-minute frame: "We have 90 minutes together. By the end, you'll have a plan and a next step. Nothing more, nothing less."
3. Screen for immediate safety: Are they in danger right now? Medical emergency? Active suicidal ideation? Acute intoxication? If yes — triage to emergency services first. This protocol begins after immediate safety is secured.
4. Begin the One-Breath Reset together: breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 2, out for 6. Three rounds. Do it with them. Not as a technique — as a human being breathing alongside another human being.

**MI alignment:** This is the Engaging task. Build trust. Establish the relationship. Set expectations.

---

### Minutes 10–25: PFD Assessment

PFD stands for Position, Fundamentals, Danger, Direction. It's the lifeguard's scan of the water before deciding how to help.

| PFD | What You're Assessing | How to Ask |
|-----|----------------------|------------|
| **P — Position** | Where are they right now? Housed or unhoused? Employed? Using or sober? In a relationship? Phone, ID, transport? | "Let's start simple. Where did you sleep last night? Do you have a phone that works? How did you get here today?" |
| **F — Fundamentals** | Are the basics met? Food, water, sleep, medication, income. | "When did you last eat? Are you on any medications right now? Are you getting any sleep?" |
| **D — Danger** | What's the immediate threat? Eviction? Withdrawal risk? Violence? Legal? Medical? | "What's the thing that feels most urgent to you right now? What keeps you up at night?" |
| **D — Direction** | What do THEY want? Not what you think they need. Their priority in their own words. | "If we could change one thing in the next 72 hours, what would matter most to you?" |

**Record the Direction answer in their exact words.** This is the most important data point in the entire intake. It tells you what they're willing to work on. Everything else flows from here.

**MI alignment:** This is the Focusing task. Establish direction — not directive, but directional. The client's own priority drives the process.

---

### Minutes 25–50: Service Navigation

Based on the PFD, you now know the most urgent needs. Start making calls. By the 30-minute mark, you should be connecting to at least one service — not after 60 minutes of paperwork.

**Service Pathway Checklist:**

| Need | Service Type | Action |
|------|-------------|--------|
| Active withdrawal risk | Medical — ER or withdrawal management | Call ahead. Arrange transport. Don't leave them alone. |
| Opioid dependence | RAAM clinic / OAT initiation | Same-day or next-day appointment. Walk-in where available. |
| Housing crisis | Shelter, transitional housing | Call current availability. Know your local beds. |
| Harm reduction | Harm reduction supplies, naloxone, safe use education | Provide kit if available. Connect to harm reduction program. |
| Income crisis | Ontario Works, ODSP, emergency funds | Initiate application if they have ID. If not, ID replacement first. |
| Mental health | Crisis team, concurrent disorders, counselling | Assess urgency. Same-day if active SI. Waitlist realities — name them honestly. |
| Peer support | Recovery meetings, peer workers | Connect to specific person/meeting, not just a phone number. |
| Legal | Legal aid, court support | Connect if active legal issue is creating barrier to everything else. |

**The worker's role here is navigator, not therapist.** You are the connective tissue between this person and the services that exist. You don't deliver therapy. You make sure they reach the right door.

**MI alignment:** This is still Focusing — connecting the client's self-identified priority to the services that can actually address it.

---

### Minutes 50–70: Substance Profile, HALT Baseline, and PAWS Positioning

Now that the person is stabilised, connected, and has a sense that something is happening — now you gather the clinical data. Not before.

**Substance Profile:**
- Primary substance(s) — what they use most
- How long they've been using (years)
- Days since they last used
- How many times they've tried to stop before
- Any overdose history
- Current medications (OAT, psychiatric, other)
- Detox status — completed, in progress, or not yet

**HALT Baseline (4 sliders, 1–5 each):**
- Hungry / Hurt — physical pain or neglect
- Angry — resentment, shame, frustration
- Lonely — disconnected, isolated
- Tired — exhausted, sleep-deprived, PAWS fatigue

**PAWS Positioning:**
Based on the substance profile, the system auto-generates the client's PAWS timeline position and top 3 active hurdles. This is the moment you turn the screen around — like turning the computer to the customer in car sales.

"Based on what you've told me, here's what your brain is likely going to do over the next few months. These are the hurdles that are most active for someone with your profile. Which of these feel true for you already?"

The client reviews the hurdles. They select what resonates. They choose which ones to learn about first. They pick the strategies that interest them. **They are building their own recovery configuration.** This is MI's Empowerment principle made tangible.

**MI alignment:** This is the Evoking task. You're not telling them what to do. You're showing them what's coming and letting them decide what matters. Their own motivation emerges from understanding what they're up against.

---

### Minutes 70–80: The 72-Hour Micro-Plan

This is the most important output of the entire session. It's built in the client's own words. Not your words. Not clinical language. Their words.

**Four fields:**

1. **"The one thing I'll do tomorrow."**
   Not five things. One thing. Completable. Specific. "I'll call the RAAM clinic at 9am." "I'll eat breakfast." "I'll text my sister."

2. **"If I feel the pull, I'll call..."**
   Name and number. Saved to quick-dial on their phone. Not a general helpline — a specific person who knows them. If they don't have one, this becomes the first service gap to address.

3. **"My next appointment is..."**
   Date, time, location. Synced to a reminder if they have a phone. Written on the pocket card if they don't.

4. **"The one thing I want different in 3 days."**
   Not a life overhaul. One thing. "I want to have slept two full nights." "I want to know where I'm staying next week." "I want to have gone one day without using."

**MI alignment:** This is the Planning task. How the person will bring about desired change — in their own words, at their own pace, toward their own goal.

---

### Minutes 80–90: Anchor and Close

Close the way you opened. With presence.

1. **Name what they accomplished:** "In 90 minutes, you did [specific thing]. That's real. You did that."

2. **Deliver the Client Handbook:** Their tools, their plan, their contacts, in their hands. Digital in the app. Print version available for anyone without reliable phone access.

3. **Schedule the first check-in:** 24 or 48 hours. Put it in both your calendars. "I'll text you tomorrow. Just a quick check. No pressure."

4. **Close with the stance:** "The shore is closer than you think. You proved that today."

5. **If they want to continue:** The PAWS monitoring layer activates. The 12 Blocks become available. The toolkit scales with their recovery stage. The communication bridge opens between you.

6. **If they're not ready for more:** The Prodigal Son Protocol applies. No guilt. No pressure. The door stays open. Their 72-hour plan is theirs. Their handbook is theirs. When they're ready, they're ready.

## After the 90 Minutes — What Activates

| Timeframe | What Happens |
|-----------|-------------|
| **24–48 hours** | First check-in. "How was last night? Did you use the plan?" |
| **Day 7** | HALT check review. Which PAWS hurdles are showing up? |
| **Day 14** | PAWS positioning review. Adjust hurdle weights. Introduce next tools. |
| **Day 30+** | Arc view begins. Block monitoring active. Pattern recognition starts. |
| **Ongoing** | Client logs between sessions. Worker sees what's consented. The relationship lives between appointments, not just during them. |

## Six Scenario Protocols

These cover situations that require deviation from the standard 90-minute flow.

**Scenario 1 — Active Medical Emergency:** Stop the protocol. Call 911. Stay with the person. LLJ resumes after medical is addressed.

**Scenario 2 — Active Suicidal Ideation:** Stop the protocol. Follow your agency's SI protocol. If none exists: stay with the person, call 988, do not leave them alone. LLJ resumes only after safety is secured.

**Scenario 3 — Active Intoxication:** The person cannot meaningfully participate. Ensure their immediate safety. Provide the pocket card with your number. "Come back when you're ready. I'll be here." No judgement. No lecture.

**Scenario 4 — No ID or Documentation:** ID replacement becomes the first service navigation priority. Many services require ID. Start there.

**Scenario 5 — Refusal of All Services:** Honour it. Provide the Client Handbook and the pocket card. "This is yours whether you use any services or not. My number is on the card. No timeline. No pressure." Prodigal Son Protocol.

**Scenario 6 — Language Barrier:** Use interpretation services. Slow down. Simplify. The PFD can be conducted with minimal language. Use visual aids, gestures, and patience. Do not skip stabilisation because communication is difficult.

## Documentation

Every completed onboarding produces one record containing:
- PFD snapshot (Position, Fundamentals, Danger, Direction — in client's words)
- Substance profile and HALT baseline
- PAWS positioning (timeline + active hurdles + client-selected priorities)
- 72-hour micro-plan (in client's exact words)
- Service pathways connected
- Check-in schedule
- Worker ID and timestamp

This is the standardised record. Every agency using this protocol produces the same data in the same structure. That's what makes it a standard — and that's what makes it improvable over time.
